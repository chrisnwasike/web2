An honest observation

What's the
actual problem?

The problems are rarely unique. Most businesses are dealing with the same issues, they've just got used to 'managing' them.

This is usually the point at which client contact me. Not because they want a new website. But because something is costing them business, and they've finally decided to fix it.
01
The site was built for appearance, not operation

It looks reasonable. But behind it, the team still uses spreadsheets, emails, and manual follow-ups to do work the site should be handling. Nobody planned the operation - they planned the pages.

02
Users drop off and nobody knows exactly where

Traffic comes in. Conversions don't match up. The form is too long, the navigation is confusing, or the page doesn't answer the question the visitor actually has. Most of this is fixable - once you look at it properly.

03
Nobody thinks about what happens after a customer arrives

The marketing spends money to get people there. The site has no clear path forward. No logical next step. No mechanism for capturing interest or starting a relationship. Traffic and conversion are treated as separate problems - they aren't.

04
The developer disappeared after launch

There was no handover. Nobody knows how to change anything. The business is dependent on someone who's no longer available, and every small update becomes a project. This is more common than it should be.

05
The site doesn't work properly on mobile

It was built on desktop and tested on desktop. Half of visitors - often more - are on phones. A layout that breaks, loads slowly, or buries key information on mobile is turning people away before they've made a decision.

06
The system was built for today, not for growth

It works at current volume. But add more users, more data, more products - and cracks appear. Features that should be straightforward require significant rework. The architecture wasn't planned. It was improvised.

07
The admin system is worse than the public site

Staff navigate it slowly. Simple tasks require multiple steps. When anything changes, someone has to call the developer. A system your own team struggles to use is an expensive problem hiding in plain sight.

"A lot of businesses are losing time and money because their systems are inefficient. The problem is usually spread across daily tasks and staff workflows, so it goes unnoticed for far too long."

- This is the first conversation I have with most new clients.
